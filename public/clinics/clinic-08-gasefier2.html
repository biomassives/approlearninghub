Convert plastic waste and wood into valuable fuels and materials through controlled thermal decomposition. Learn to build, operate, and safely manage small-scale pyrolysis systems that can transform problematic waste into useful products while reducing landfill volume.

Technical Overview

Pyrolysis is the thermal decomposition of materials at elevated temperatures in an inert atmosphere. It involves a change of chemical composition and is irreversible. The word is derived from the Greek words 'pyro' meaning fire and 'lysis' meaning breakdown. For plastic waste and wood, pyrolysis typically occurs at temperatures between 400-600°C without the presence of oxygen.

Performance Metrics

Temperature Range
400-600°C (plastic), 300-800°C (wood)
Yield Ratios (Plastic)
Oil: 45-75%, Gas: 15-30%, Char: 5-25% (varies by plastic type)
Yield Ratios (Wood)
Biochar: 20-35%, Bio-oil: 30-50%, Gas: 20-30%
Energy Requirements
Approximately 1-2 MJ/kg of material processed (after initial heating)

Pyrolysis Process Stages

1
Pre-Processing (Preparation)

Material is cleaned, sorted, and sized appropriately. For plastics, this involves washing, drying, and shredding. For wood, this involves chipping or splitting to appropriate sizes and drying to reduce moisture content.

2
Heating (200-300°C)

The material is heated in an oxygen-free environment, causing the release of moisture and some volatile compounds. This stage prepares the material for the main pyrolysis process.

3
Primary Pyrolysis (300-600°C)

Chemical bonds begin to break down due to the heat, releasing a mixture of gases and vapors. The material undergoes significant decomposition, producing volatile compounds that leave behind solid char.

4
Secondary Reactions (400-800°C)

Vapors released during primary pyrolysis undergo further reactions, breaking down into lighter molecules. These reactions affect the final composition of the liquid and gaseous products.

5
Condensation & Collection

The hot vapors are cooled in condensers, causing heavier molecules to liquefy into pyrolysis oil. Non-condensable gases are collected separately, and solid char remains in the reaction chamber.

Advantages & Disadvantages

Advantages
  • Reduces waste volume by 90% or more
  • Converts problematic waste into usable products
  • Creates multiple valuable outputs (oils, gas, char)
  • Can process mixed plastic waste with some limitations
  • Produces less emissions than open burning of waste
  • Biochar from wood pyrolysis is valuable for soil improvement
Disadvantages
  • Requires significant heat input to start and maintain the process
  • Oils produced need further refining for many applications
  • Some plastics produce hazardous byproducts (especially PVC)
  • Requires careful temperature control and monitoring
  • Small-scale systems have lower efficiency than industrial ones

Practical Applications

Cooking Fuel

Using condensed oils or gases as alternative cooking fuels. The oil can be burned in modified stoves to provide clean, efficient cooking heat.

Heating

Burning pyrolysis products for space or water heating. The consistent energy content makes it suitable for various heating applications.

Engine Fuel

With refining, oils can be used in some engines and generators. Further processing can create fuel similar to diesel or kerosene.

Biochar

Wood-derived charcoal for soil improvement and carbon sequestration. Biochar improves soil fertility, water retention, and can store carbon for hundreds of years.

Wood Vinegar

Liquid byproduct from wood pyrolysis used in agriculture as a natural pesticide, growth stimulant, and soil conditioner.

Key System Designs

Batch Retort System

Sealed container designs for controlled thermal decomposition. Simplest design that processes one batch of material at a time.

Continuous Feed Design

Systems that allow ongoing feeding and extraction. More complex but offers higher throughput for larger waste volumes.

Condensation Methods

Techniques to capture and separate liquid byproducts. Critical for maximizing oil yield and quality from the pyrolysis process.
